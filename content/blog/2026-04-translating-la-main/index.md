## Introduction

As I work through translating Maupassant's stories, I will not typically be reading other translations or comparing them to my own. But these early comparative notes on one story, "The Hand" (_La main_) — just the first page of it, actually — may be useful as a motivating example: to show a little bit of what I feel is _qualitatively_ missing{{ sidenote(num="1", body='I&#39;ve already written, and will have more to say, about what&#39;s missing quantitatively — see <a href="https://myersm0.github.io/maupassant-study/blog/2026-02-intro-to-isosceles-1/#maupassant-mcmaster">here</a>, for example.') }} in existing Maupassant translations, and at the same time to demonstrate some of the translation principles that I hope will characterize my own work.

## Setting the scene

"The Hand" begins with a framing prologue in which Monsieur Bermutier, a _juge d'instruction_, addresses a group of women seated around the fireplace. At first his topic is a well-known contemporary crime, "the mysterious Saint-Cloud case," in which he evidently was not involved himself — rather he offers his take on the popular theories surrounding it. The women are captivated by the mystery. One of them mentions the supernatural; this is the pivot that brings Bermutier to introduce another topic, a case in which he _was_ directly involved, and his account of that case will make up the remainder of the story.

But what is a _juge d'instruction_? From Littré's French dictionary:

> Examining magistrate — a magistrate appointed to investigate crimes and offenses, gather evidence or indications, and have suspects arrested and questioned.{{ sidenote(num="2", body="Juge d'instruction, magistrat établi pour rechercher les crimes et les délits, en recueillir les preuves ou indices, et faire arrêter et interroger les prévenus.") }}

Both Sandra Smith and the McMaster et al. translate his title is simply as "judge." This is imprecise and renders an inadequate impression of the man who's about to become the narrator of the story we're reading. Specifically, it fails to underscore the contrast that takes us from the framing prologue into the story proper: the contrast of a man discussing opinions second-hand on a popular contemporary case, and the same man discussing another case in which he was deeply involved in an investigative capacity.

## The clean-shaven mouth

The third paragraph opens with an image that's hard to render well in English:

> Plusieurs femmes s’étaient levées pour s’approcher et demeuraient debout, l’œil fixé sur la bouche rasée du magistrat d’où sortaient les paroles graves.

The main problem is _"la bouche rasée"_: literally "the clean-shaven mouth," but we would never say that in English. _"Le paroles graves"_ — the words from Bermutier are weighty, authoritative and anatomically localized, coming from that same clean-shaven mouth. McMaster et al. reduce the anatomical specificity to "clean-shaven _face_," thereby dropping the connection between mouth and words:

> Some women had risen, in order to get nearer to him, and were standing with their eyes fastened on the clean-shaven face of the judge, who was saying such weighty things.

Smith makes the same shift, but in addition she diffuses the "solemn words" (_paroles graves_) into _speech_, and their character is downgraded to a matter of tone rather than of content too:

> Several women got up and went to stand closer to him, concentrating on the judge's clean-shaven face as he spoke in a serious tone of voice.

In my translation I aimed to keep the image and the connection between mouth and words coming from it, at the expense of having to slightly belabor the phrasing into a pair of observations — as if zooming in from the _jaw_ to the _mouth_:

> Several women had risen to come closer, and they gathered watching the magistrate's clean-shaven jaw, the mouth issuing such grave words.

## _Le fantastique_

Let's consider the exact line at which Bermutier introduces his case that will soon become the story's focus. In the original French, he says to the women:
> "Mais j’ai eu, moi, autrefois, à suivre une affaire où vraiment semblait se mêler quelque chose de fantastique."

My own rendering of this sentence is almost literal:
> "But I personally had to investigate a case once in which there truly seemed to be something of the fantastic at work."

There are at least three elements of this sentence in the French that I thought it important to preserve: 
1. An emphasis on his personal involvement (_"j'ai eu, moi"_ → "I personally")
2. His juxtaposition of "truly" and "seemed" (_"vraiment semblait"_)
3. His precise word choice — the fantastic (_"fantastique"_) — and its placement at the end of the sentence for emphasis

Let's consider Smith's translation:
> "However, I did once, long ago, know of a case where something truly inexplicable seemed to be involved."

And the McMaster et al.:
> "But once I had to take charge of an affair in which the uncanny seemed to play a part."

Neither of these allow Bermutier that key word, _fantastique_, which he's provocatively introducing here only to walk it back a few paragraphs later (as we'll see soon). McMaster's "uncanny" is a decent substitute, but his sentence still loses two of the three elements above: the emphatic personal involvement is gone, and "seemed to play a part" doesn't adequately register the tension of _vraiment semblait_ — the insistence and the hedge. Smith's "inexplicable" is further off the mark, and weakens the tenor of Bermutier's rhetoric in a way that compounds a few paragraphs later.

_Fantastique_ here is the word Bermutier is willing to use before he retreats to safer ground two paragraphs later:

> "N’allez pas croire, au moins, que j’aie pu, même un instant, supposer en cette aventure quelque chose de ***surhumain***. Je ne crois qu’aux causes normales. Mais si, au lieu d’employer le mot ***« surnaturel »*** pour exprimer ce que nous ne comprenons pas, nous nous servions simplement du mot ***« inexplicable »***, cela vaudrait beaucoup mieux."

I've emphasized three words in the above: superhuman (_surhumain_), supernatural, and inexplicable. Smith and McMaster et al. both conflate superhuman and supernatural into just the latter term. But what Bermutier is doing is precise: he first rejects _surhumain_ agency, then questions the category _supernatural_, and finally proposes to replace it with _inexplicable_.

In outline: consider the delicacy of Bermutier's rationist stance. First, a couple paragraphs before, he reaches for a provocative, atmospheric word, _fantastique_; then spends the next paragraph walking it back through _surhumain_ and _surnaturel_ until he arrives at the safely neutral _inexplicable_. When Smith gives him "inexplicable" from the start, that arc disappears. Bermutier becomes internally consistent — a man who coolly introduces something _inexplicable_, then recommends that same word. But the Bermutier of the original text is a man who says more than he means to, and then tries to unsay it. That's lost in Smith and garbled in McMaster et al.

## A note on translations

None of this is to suggest that my own translations are free of compromise. Even in these cherry-picked examples, you may find places where an image has been changed a little, or some phrasing made more explicit, or a rhythm adjusted to remain idiomatic in English. It's largely a balancing act of competing priorities: sense, tone, structure, and rhetorical effect. In general, however, my choices are guided by a careful reading of the originals and a commitment to preserving Maupassant’s intentions, micro and macro.
